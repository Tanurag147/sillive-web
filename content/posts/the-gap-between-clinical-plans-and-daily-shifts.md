A Behaviour Support Plan is, on average, 47 pages long. It costs the participant's NDIS plan thousands of dollars to produce. It's written by a clinician who has spent years training to assess behaviour, design interventions, and write recommendations.

And then it sits in a folder.

This is the most expensive failure mode in SIL operations. It's also the one no one wants to talk about, because the alternative — admitting that brilliant clinical work is being routinely ignored at the shift floor — is uncomfortable for everyone in the chain.

## Where the plan goes after it's written

Walk a BSP through the operational chain inside a typical SIL home and watch what happens.

The BSP arrives in the provider's inbox as a PDF attachment. The director reads the executive summary, files it in a participant folder, and forwards it to the house lead. The house lead skims it, prints it, puts it in the participant's binder. Done.

Then the night shift comes on. Sarah, who's worked in the home for two months, has never read the BSP. She doesn't know there's a recommendation about verbal-only prompting during meal preparation. She doesn't know the participant has a sensory sensitivity to fluorescent overhead light after 7pm.

She does her best. She follows the routine she's been shown. She writes a shift note: "All well. Dinner at 6:30pm. TV until 9. Bed by 10."

The plan was excellent. The shift was fine. The recommendations went unimplemented. Nobody noticed.

## Why this happens

Three reasons, none of them about worker quality.

**The clinical document and the shift document are different objects.** The BSP is a 47-page PDF. The shift document is a 4-line text note. There's no shared structure, no shared vocabulary, no link between them. A worker can't reference a BSP recommendation they've never seen.

**Clinical recommendations are written for the clinician, not the worker.** "Implement positive behaviour support strategies emphasising replacement behaviours during transitions" makes sense to a behaviour analyst. To a 22-year-old support worker on her third week of casual shifts, it's noise.

**No one closes the loop.** The clinician who wrote the BSP doesn't see whether it was implemented. The provider doesn't track which recommendations are being delivered. The support coordinator finds out at the next annual review — by which point twelve months of implementation has either happened or not happened, and there's no way to know.

## What "closing the gap" actually looks like

Here's what we do at Caring Hands SA. It's not the only way. It's the way that works.

When a new clinical document arrives — BSP, OT report, speech pathology plan — we extract the actionable recommendations within 48 hours. Not summarised. Not paraphrased. Lifted out as discrete actions, each one tagged with its source document and section number.

Each action is then **routed to the shift briefing** for the relevant participant. So when Sarah arrives for her 6am shift, she opens the briefing on her phone and sees:

> **For Aizezi today:**
> - Verbal prompts only during meal prep — source: BSP §4.2
> - Sensory check before 7pm if using lounge — source: OT recs §3
> - Practice asking for help with shoes — source: speech path §2.1

That's not a 47-page document. That's three actionable items. Sarah can read it in 90 seconds. She can do the work.

At the end of the shift, she ticks off what she actually did and writes a short note against each one. The note links back to the BSP automatically. The audit evidence builds itself. The next shift gets continuity. The coordinator can see, in real time, that the plan they wrote is reaching the floor.

## The unglamorous truth

This isn't sophisticated. There's no magic. The gap between clinical plans and daily shifts is closed by one boring discipline: **structured extraction of recommendations into a shift-readable format, every time, for every participant.**

Most providers don't do it because it's tedious manual work for a small operator and impossible at scale. So the recommendations sit in folders. The plans get reviewed annually. The audit trail gets back-filled.

If you want to close the gap, you need a system that does the extraction and routing for you. That's what we built. But the discipline is the same whether you build it, buy it, or do it by hand: every clinical recommendation needs to live in the shift briefing, or it doesn't live anywhere that matters.
