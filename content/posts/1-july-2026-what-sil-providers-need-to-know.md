The 1 July 2026 changes are not a documentation upgrade. They're a shift in what kind of evidence the NDIS Commission expects to see when it walks into your home. If you're running SIL — whether one home or twenty — you've got roughly six weeks to close three specific gaps.

This is what I'm doing inside Caring Hands SA. Take what's useful.

## What's actually changing

The headline that gets repeated in every NDIS newsletter is "stronger evidence requirements." That's true but useless. The real change is more specific.

Auditors are now expected to verify that **clinical recommendations from approved plans are being implemented at the shift level** — not just that the plans exist, not just that workers were rostered, not just that incidents were documented. The implementation itself has to leave a trail.

If your current evidence chain is "we have the BSP" → "we ran the shift" → "no incidents reported," that's no longer enough. The missing middle is "we delivered against the BSP recommendations on this specific shift, by this specific worker."

## The three gaps most providers will fail on

### 1. Clinical-to-shift traceability

Most providers can show that a Behaviour Support Plan exists. Almost none can show that recommendation §4.2 of that plan was actively implemented during last Tuesday's afternoon shift.

This isn't a paperwork problem. It's that no one in the operation is connecting a clinical document to a daily shift action. The BSP lives in a folder. The shift note lives in a different system. There's no thread between them.

### 2. Continuity of support across shift handovers

The 1 July guidance specifically calls out continuity. Auditors will look for evidence that critical information from one shift reached the next — not "we had a meeting once a fortnight," but evidence at the handover point itself.

Verbal handovers don't count. Email summaries don't count if no one read them. What counts is structured handover artefacts the next worker actually opened.

### 3. Coordinator-visible implementation

Support coordinators are increasingly being asked, in audit interviews, what they know about implementation of the plans they wrote. If their answer is "I check in at the annual review," that's a problem — for them and for the provider they're connected to.

The expectation is that coordinators have ongoing visibility, not retrospective visibility.

## What to do before 1 July

You don't need to buy software to fix this. But you do need to do four things.

**One.** Map every active clinical recommendation across every participant onto a single sheet. Not the PDFs — the actual recommendations, extracted as bullet points. If you can't articulate the list in 20 minutes, your workers definitely can't follow it.

**Two.** Pick five recommendations that should be visible on every shift briefing. Make them part of the shift document the worker opens at the start of the shift. Not at the end. At the start.

**Three.** At handover, require the outgoing worker to mark which of those five were addressed and which weren't. This becomes your audit evidence chain.

**Four.** Send the running total to the support coordinator weekly. One paragraph: "Here's what we delivered against the plan this week." If they push back on receiving it, they're not the right coordinator for an audit-ready operation.

## The honest part

If you're trying to do this in spreadsheets and shared folders, you can — for one home. At two homes the cracks show. At three homes you'll miss something important and the audit will find it.

This is the gap SILLIVE was built to close. We're not the only way to close it. But you do need to close it before 1 July, and the four steps above are the minimum.
