Three letters. Three steps. Eighteen months of getting it wrong before getting it right.

ERA — Extract, Route, Apply — is not a methodology I invented. It's the operational pattern that emerged after I tried, and abandoned, every other way of running a SIL home.

This is the design story.

## The wrong frameworks I tried first

When I started Caring Hands SA in early 2024, I came in with the business-school instinct of trying to find an existing framework that would do the heavy lifting. I tried a few. None of them survived contact with a real morning shift.

**The "policy-first" approach.** Write everything down. Procedures for every scenario. Get workers to read the manual. This produced a 200-page document and zero behaviour change. Workers don't have time to read manuals on shift.

**The "incident-driven" approach.** Wait for things to go wrong, then write a corrective action. This produced a tidy compliance binder and a steady stream of preventable incidents. The clinical recommendations that would have prevented them were never read.

**The "rostering-first" approach.** Use a rostering platform and assume that the right worker, at the right time, will deliver the right care. This produced excellent shift coverage and almost no clinical implementation. Coverage is necessary, not sufficient.

Each of these failed in the same way: they treated the operation as a coverage problem when it's actually a translation problem.

## What the operation actually needs to do

Every morning, I face the same question: did the right thing happen in the home overnight, against what was supposed to happen?

For that question to have a meaningful answer, three things need to be true.

1. The "supposed to happen" needs to be specific. Not "follow the BSP." Specific actions, tied to specific recommendations, for specific participants on specific days.
2. Those actions need to reach the worker who'll be on shift. Not in a binder. In their hand, before they walk in.
3. What actually happened needs to be captured against what was supposed to happen — not as a separate exercise, but inside the same workflow.

That's three steps. Extract. Route. Apply. ERA.

## Extract

Extract is the step where a clinical document — usually a PDF — becomes a structured list of actions. Not a summary. Not bullet points for the director. Discrete actions, each one attributable to a source document and a clinician.

Done well, this means a 47-page Behaviour Support Plan becomes maybe 12 actionable items. Each one says what to do, when to do it, what to watch for, and which document it came from.

Done badly, this is where most providers stop. They "extract" by writing a one-page summary and filing it. That's not extraction. That's compression. Compression isn't what the worker needs.

The hardest part of Extract is **resisting the urge to interpret**. A worker doesn't need my interpretation of what the clinician meant. The worker needs the clinician's recommendation, intact, with my job being only to make it discoverable at shift time.

## Route

Route is the step where the extracted actions land where the work happens — in the shift briefing, on the worker's phone, before the shift starts.

The decision that makes this work is that the briefing is **the only document the worker needs to read before shift**. Not the BSP. Not the OT report. Not the standing orders. The briefing, which is two pages on a phone screen, contains everything that matters for the next eight hours.

Behind the scenes, the briefing is assembled from the extracted action library. The participant's active goals get pulled in. Recent overnight events get pulled in. Standing recommendations get pulled in. The worker sees a coherent document; we see a routing system that draws from the right sources every time.

Route is the step that makes Extract worthwhile. Without it, the extracted actions sit in a database that nobody opens.

## Apply

Apply is the step where the worker captures what actually happened — and where the audit evidence trail is built, passively, as a side-effect of running the shift.

When Sarah finishes her shift, she goes through the briefing she opened at the start. For each action, she ticks it off (delivered) or notes why not (didn't apply, participant declined, time ran out). She adds short context for anything noteworthy.

That's it. That's the entire end-of-shift workflow. It takes four minutes. The audit trail forms itself.

The thing that took me longest to understand is that Apply is **not a separate documentation task**. The whole point is that the shift note and the implementation evidence are the same artefact. If they're separate, workers will skip one of them. They always do.

## Why the order matters

People sometimes ask why ERA isn't RAE or AER. The order is load-bearing.

Extract has to come first because routing without structured actions is just forwarding emails. Route has to come second because applying without routing means the worker is doing extraction on the fly, which they won't. Apply has to come third because it depends on both.

You can do Extract without Route and Apply, and you'll have a beautiful unused database. You can do Route and Apply without Extract, and you'll have shift notes that are disconnected from clinical intent — which is what most providers already have.

The whole sequence has to run, every cycle, for every participant. That's what software is for.

## What it feels like when it's working

The morning of a Tuesday in March 2026, Caring Hands SA. I open my morning summary email at 6:14am over coffee.

> Aizezi · Quiet overnight, settled by 22:40. Engagement: cooking video with Lily. Goals supported: 2 of 3 (meal prep ✓, social outing ✓, hydration check missed). Support delivered: 1:1 ratio held. **Needs your attention: GP follow-up form due Friday.**

That paragraph is built from an Apply step that happened at 06:00, drawing on Routes that happened at 22:00 and 06:00, drawing on Extracts that happened weeks ago when the BSP and OT reports were processed.

I read it in 12 seconds. I know the home is fine. I know what needs me today. I make the next decision informed.

That's ERA. Three letters. Three steps. Built inside a SIL home, for SIL homes, by an operator who got tired of running blind.
